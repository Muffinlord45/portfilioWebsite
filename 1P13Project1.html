Overview

Our team's scenario was a humanitarian aid mission to the city of Quetzaltenango in Guatemala with the Engineers Without Borders. As the task described, we were to design low cost, simple, and durable wind turbines capable of producing enough electricity to power devices like lights and simple appliances. Additionally, the turbines should be able to be produced by locals with rudimentary tools, and be made from locally-soured materials. Hence, our objective was to design a turbine that can produce electricity reliably, with the core principles of low cost, simplicity, and weather resistance.

Function

  • Generate energy for simple
    electrical devices.

Objectives

  • Cost should be minimized.
  • Manufacturing time/energy should be minimized

Constraint

  • Blade deflection must be between 8.5mm and 10mm.

Material Selection

A simple decision matrix we made to compare our top three materials

The biggest issue we encountered when designing the blade was trying to decide on a material to choose for the blade to be made out of. We made a decision matrix comparing steel, wood, and bamboo and got a two-way tie between steel and bamboo.

- Bamboo would be able to be grown locally by anyone in their backyard, but it would fall victim to the weather over time.

- Steel on the other hand would need to be sourced from a local manufacturer, but had the upside of being cheaper and much more durable than bamboo is.

- In the end we chose Steel for the aforementioned reasons of low cost, corrosion resistance, and availability. As we were to minimize cost, the price of materials outweighed other considerations.

Challenges Encountered

What They Were

As with most team projects, there were several issues that came up during this one, primarily stemming from poor communication. Since we only relied on one method of communication (Microsoft Teams), having one person not do their assigned work and subsequently not respond was extremely frustrating.

Additionally, as this was the first project where we had to use Autodesk Inventor, the time it took to design the blade and by extension, the bonus I made, was significantly higher than I would have liked it to be.

How They Were Overcame

As a team, we eventually decided to keep using Microsoft Teams, but also to increase the number of weekly meetings from once a week to twice a week, just in case of another "not responding" incident.

As for the issues with Autodesk Inventor, I employed a combination of YouTube tutorials and perseverance to get through my struggles, and ultimately learn to improve for the future.

What I Contributed

- Single handedly designed the turbine for the "Blow Me Away" challenge as seen below. This increased my proficiency with Inventor and CAD in general as I had to employ a number of new techniques I hadn't yet learned to achieve my vision of what the turbine should be.

- Conducted the deflection/stress analysis in Autodesk Inventor for our turbine blade. From this, I gained insight into how computer software can help predict the behavior of different materials under certain loads and how to optimize designs accordingly.

- Made the decision matrix for the material selection. In doing so, I learned a technique to narrow potential choices for a given problem down in a more sophisticated manner than just absolute "pros and cons". Furthermore, I became more familiar with material selections though the use of Granta.

- Wrote the majority of our final technical report. This allowed me to learn how to write reports in a technical field like engineering, a key aspect of any job in the field. I gained a deeper understanding of IEEE citations and employed software like Zotero to streamline the process.
